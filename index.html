01 My Journey in Hospitality
Why I fell for this industry, and why I'm not ready to stop working in it.

When I started at Life Fitness in mid-2024, I knew commercial fitness. I did not yet know hospitality. Two years later, I can tell you that hospitality stole the show. This industry is the rare category where the product and the feeling are the same thing. A room is never just a room. A gym is never just a gym. Every square foot is a signal about what the guest is worth to the brand.

What I learned, mostly through spending time with operators and sellers, is that the modern hotel is quietly rebuilding itself around wellbeing. Not as a checkbox amenity, but as a reason someone chooses one flag over another. I spent two years helping operators reframe the fitness floor as part of the guest experience rather than a box to fill, and watching that shift from product-centric to operator-centric to guest-centric is what made me fall in love with this space.

Which brings me here. The Marketing Manager role on the World of Hyatt global team sits at exactly the intersection I've been circling: a brand with a loyalty program that treats members like people, a team that is genuinely consumer-minded, and a company whose purpose is to care for people so they can be their best. I would very much like to be part of that.

02 What I've Learned About This Industry

Four things that shape how I'd approach loyalty marketing for a brand like Hyatt.

i.

Guests don't buy rooms. They buy the version of themselves they become on the trip.

The brief that opens a campaign shouldn't ask what the property offers. It should ask what the member leaves feeling. Every asset, email, and partnership either reinforces that feeling or it doesn't. Loyalty programs can live or die on whether the brand gets this right.

ii.

Wellness is no longer an amenity. It's a decision driver.

When I ran VOC calls with hospitality sellers, one pattern was unmistakable: guests now book based on whether the hotel supports their routine. Fitness, sleep, nutrition, and recovery are brand signals. A World of Hyatt member planning a week in a new city is evaluating wellbeing alongside price and points.

iii.

Luxury, Premium, and Select are different planets.

I spent two years building separate messaging strategies for three tiers of the same category. The guest at a Park Hyatt is solving a different problem than the guest at a Hyatt Place, and the campaign that speaks to both in the same voice will land with neither. Segmentation is a creative discipline before it's a data one.

iv.

CapEx decisions are yearly. Guest expectations shift quarterly.

Operators make slow, expensive bets on behalf of guests whose tastes are moving fast. Marketing's job is to bridge that gap by making the brand feel current even when the infrastructure isn't. Content, campaigns, and partnerships are how you keep a decade-old asset feeling contemporary.

The hotels that are winning right now are the ones treating fitness, sleep, and wellbeing as narrative material, not floor plans.
— From a positioning framework I built in 2025
03 The Hyatt Chapter

I've already had the pleasure of working with your team, and loved it.

One of the projects I'm proudest of from my time at Life Fitness is the customer showcase we produced at Hyatt Regency Chicago. I owned it end to end: planned the shoot, wrote the brief, coordinated testimonial interviews, and directed on-site filming.

What made it memorable wasn't the video. It was the team. Chris Carter and the renovation crew at Hyatt Regency Chicago were thoughtful, generous with their time, and clear-eyed about what they were building. It was a reminder that when the operator gets it right, the marketing almost writes itself.

04 What I'd Bring

Four things you'd get on day one.

i.

Brief-to-launch orchestration in matrixed environments.

I've written the brief, aligned the stakeholders, and shipped the campaign across social, content, email, web, and trade media. "A Stay That Moves You" was my first full-funnel integrated campaign, built from a blank page through a positioning shift the team had been circling for years.

ii.

First-party data and personalization that actually moves the needle.

I built a funnel-stage tracking system in Pardot that replaced one-size-fits-all sends with behavior-triggered, stage-appropriate communication across a 50,000-contact database. One targeted personalization campaign generated $670K+ in attributed pipeline.

iii.

A consumer-minded eye for brand work.

My background is B2B, but the muscle I've built is the one World of Hyatt needs: sharp segmentation, narrative discipline, and the ability to take a strategic point of view and translate it into work that feels human. Luxury, Premium, and Select each got their own voice on my watch.

iv.

A practical, applied use of GenAI.

To accelerate concept-to-creation without agency spend, I built custom AI agents modeled after ad agency roles — strategist, copywriter, creative director — and used them to go from brief to first-draft assets in a fraction of the usual timeline. Less "uses ChatGPT," more a real agentic workflow that made a small team move like a larger one.
